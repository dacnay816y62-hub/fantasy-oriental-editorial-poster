# Editorial systems

Choose one system per page. The system controls source choice, hierarchy, crop logic, and title role.

## 1. Painting as evidence

Use for painting, heritage, landscape, season, city memory, or literary themes.

- **Source:** one public-domain painting or handscroll scan, plus one crop from the same source.
- **Structure:** the main crop occupies 45–70%; a smaller detail appears in a non-adjacent field.
- **Title:** giant Song/Ming type, vertical or edge-cropped. Calligraphy is optional.
- **Rhythm:** paper margin, date/English label, one mineral or ochre block.
- **Avoid:** using a whole scenic painting as a polite background with title beside it.

## 2. Document and inscription

Use for text, books, calligraphy, research, publishing, architecture, or history.

- **Source:** a stone rubbing, book page, printed fragment, map, or archive document.
- **Structure:** one enlarged document fragment; another appears as a faded trace or cropped strip.
- **Title:** one real character sample can act as anchor; other characters stay typeset.
- **Rhythm:** thin rules, numbered block, grid edge, bilingual metadata column.
- **Avoid:** covering the page in seals or illegible pseudo-writing.

## 3. Object and material study

Use for craft, objects, food, tea, furniture, design, materials, or a brand with a cultural core.

- **Source:** museum object image, original close material study, or tool/illustration.
- **Structure:** an oversized hard crop plus one tight macro or silhouette.
- **Title:** quiet typeset title or a restrained sampled character; the object remains primary.
- **Rhythm:** labels, scale relation, one accent plate, broad quiet field.
- **Avoid:** product-ad lighting, full isolated packshot, and generic luxury gloss.

## 4. Image inside type

Use for a strong concept, a short title, or city/cultural identity.

- **Source:** a single image/print source with readable internal texture.
- **Structure:** one oversized typeset Chinese character becomes a crop window; supporting title characters sit outside.
- **Title:** typeset for exact readability; use sampled calligraphy only as a secondary interference.
- **Rhythm:** one aligned information column, a small index block, and source-image echo.
- **Avoid:** filling every character; one dominant type-mask is enough.

## 5. Night edition

Use for music, fashion, immersive culture, night events, luxury, or an atmospheric material narrative.

- **Source:** dark-ground material image, archival black-and-white image, lacquer, textile, or original still life.
- **Structure:** one lit fragment against a large black field; second detail is small and low contrast.
- **Title:** pale Song/Ming or a single restrained calligraphic glyph.
- **Rhythm:** narrow warm accent, fine rules, precise small label.
- **Avoid:** gold effects, red-and-gold festivity, and black background as a shortcut for “high-end.”

## Reference DNA

The desired visual language is not “Chinese style.” It is contemporary editorial recontextualisation:

- real cultural material becomes a crop, a scan, a mask, or a piece of evidence;
- title and image create intentional pressure at edges;
- small information groups create a reading rhythm;
- typography alternates between evidence (sampled glyph) and precision (typeset text);
- texture belongs to a source, rather than being applied globally;
- each cover has a distinct internal logic.

## Copy-block rule

Small type must carry meaning, not simulate editorial density. Use one coherent sentence or short paragraph tied to the subject, a real caption, or exact supplied metadata. Never scatter one-character fragments, random English, pseudo-labels, or unreadable filler around blank space. If the user gives no copy, write one restrained non-factual thematic line and keep it legible.

## Anti-repetition rule

Do not let every poster become a giant-title composition. In iterative testing, vary both the editorial system and the title-design strategy:

- one poster may use a large edge-cropped title;
- the next should use a quiet catalogue title, a split inscription, a type-specimen field, an object-label title, or a dense text-field rhythm;
- if two posters share the same title scale, they must differ in title placement, image dominance, metadata rhythm, and source role;
- a regional series may share a family resemblance, but each city needs a distinct typographic behavior tied to its evidence.

## Reference is grammar, not a template

Before using a supplied poster as a reference, identify its working relationships: which glyph carries structure, which holds imagery, where the readable title sits, how blank space functions, and how small information sets the rhythm. Those relationships can be useful; their coordinates are not a layout to copy.

For every new output, keep only the subject-relevant relationships. Change at least three structural variables from the reference: title role or scale, image role, dominant axis, reading path, information placement, palette, crop logic, or editorial system. If those changes cannot be named, redesign before generating.
